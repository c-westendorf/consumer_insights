Part I · Product Definition — What This Is

Today, answering the question "what happened last week?" costs three to five analyst days and produces a one-time deliverable. The same question asked next week costs the same. Nothing compounds.

This product changes that structural equation. It is a governed insight product — a conversational system that delivers reusable, attributed answers to a defined class of enterprise questions. Every governed answer makes the next one cheaper to produce. Every question-answer pair improves the mapping from question to data. Every reconciliation resolved makes the next one faster.

The output is not a report. It is not a dashboard. It is a governed, reusable answer — one that permanently removes a class of recurring questions from the analyst queue and improves every cycle it runs.

The user experience is a blank canvas that becomes a conversation. The executive arrives, asks a question or states a hypothesis, and the product responds — in plain prose, with a point of view, with a generated recommendation for where to go next. The experience feels like talking to a brilliant analyst who has already done the work. Because it has.

The marginal cost of insight approaches zero. That is the structural shift. That is what this product is.

This document defines the interaction logic that makes that experience possible — and the boundaries that protect it from becoming something else.
Part I Continued · Product Boundaries

The IS / IS NOT Reference Grid

These distinctions are not aesthetic preferences. Each "is not" represents a scope drift risk that would compromise the compounding value model at the core of this product. When a stakeholder requests a feature that pulls toward one of these shapes, this grid is the answer.

This product IS
This product IS NOT
A governed insight product Reusable, attributed answers to a defined class of enterprise questions. Every answer improves the next one. The system compounds.
A dashboard Dashboards display data. This product delivers governed conclusions. The VP should never have to interpret a visualization — they should receive an interpretation.
A conversational interface with a point of view The product speaks. It leads with findings, not options. It recommends next moves. It has already done the analytical thinking before the executive reads the response.
A reporting layer Reporting surfaces data. This product surfaces implications. The distinction is the "so what" — a reporting layer leaves that to the reader. This product owns it.
A decision support instrument Every response is calibrated to the executive's decision mode and classified by decision tier — in-week tactical, quarterly strategic, or multi-year capital.
A query tool Query tools answer the question asked. This product answers the question asked and surfaces the next relevant question. The executive never has to know what to ask next.
A trust transfer mechanism The product earns the executive's trust by being consistently right about what matters, transparent about confidence, and accurate about decision tier classification. Trust is the product.
An analyst replacement Analysts do open-ended causal reasoning. This product reports directional statistics with signal strength. It removes recurring structured questions from the analyst queue — not the analyst.
A compounding system Every governed metric makes the next metric cheaper. Every reconciliation resolved makes the next faster. Every workflow captured makes the next automatable. The value grows with use.
A 2-year platform build The POC delivers a working governed insight product for a defined question class. Scope is bounded. The compounding architecture scales from a working foundation, not a theoretical one.
Part II · Interaction Specification — Response Generation

Response Anatomy

Every response is governed by a five-layer template grounded in a customer growth framework. The template is a generation constraint, not a display format. The product knows how to reason across all five layers. What appears in any given response is determined by entry mode and decision depth — not by the template itself. Layers appear only when they are doing work.

Layer
What it contains
When it appears
Layer 1
Frame
Customer segment, value measurement, state
Who, what, where. The governed scope of this response. Which customer population, which value measurement, what time window. Establishes the subject before any claim is made. Makes the answer repeatable and trustworthy.
Always
Every response begins with a governed frame. Without it, the answer cannot be attributed, forwarded, or trusted. Even a brief hypothesis validation response names its scope.
Layer 2
Observable Outcomes
Lagging behaviors, baseline, persistence
What was observed. Performance signal against comparative periods (WoW, MoM, YoY). Stickiness of the behavior — is this a one-week blip or a structural shift persisting across periods? Stickiness is the key distinction.
Always in open exploration
Present in directed and hypothesis modes when the question is about what happened. May be abbreviated when the executive's question focuses on drivers rather than outcomes.
Layer 3
Drivers
Directional statistics, signal strength, early warning
Attribution explanations for observed outcomes. Directional statistics only — no causal claims. The 11 attribution dimensions are checked internally. Only the dimensions with sufficient signal strength surface here. "Associated with" not "caused by."
Conditional
Appears when attribution has been resolved to a meaningful level of signal. Omitted when the data is directionally clear at the outcome level and attribution adds no decision value.
Layer 4
Business Value
Enterprise outcomes quantified
What layers 2 and 3 mean in value terms. Converts behavioral observation into financial or strategic stake. Answers "how much does this matter" before the executive has to ask. Anchors the finding to the four strategic pillars.
Conditional
Appears when the value quantification is available and material. In POC phase, appears when the data supports it. Never fabricated or estimated without explicit confidence labeling.
Layer 5
Implication
Current state, opportunity, early warnings, intervention, decision tier
The "so what." Five components: what we observe now, what we can do, what leading indicators to watch, what proactive actions are available with expected directional impact, and which decision tier(s) this finding belongs to. A finding can span tiers — with distinct actions at each level.
Conditional on decision mode
Full implication layer appears in outcome-focused mode. Abbreviated in directional mode. In hypothesis validation mode, limited to the components that bear on the hypothesis being tested.
The 11 attribution dimensions — trip type (front store vs Rx vs PCW), channel (in-store vs digital), channel mix (ecom / BOPIS / drive-through), basket type (full price vs discounted), shopper type (front store, Rx, PCW, non-loyalty), trip deciles, behavior/outcome segments, lifecycle stage (new, active, lapsing, churned), region, urban density, trip mission — are internal grading criteria. They are a completeness check, not a navigation system. The product grades itself against all 11 before committing to a finding. Only load-bearing dimensions appear in the response.
Response Title

Every response begins with a title that is an actionable finding or a named tension. It is never a description of what the response covers. The title is the product's point of view, stated before any supporting evidence.

✓ Actionable finding title
"Lapsing loyalty members in urban stores are driving the front store trip decline"
States the finding, identifies the population, names the location. Implies an action without prescribing it.
~ Named tension title
"Front store trips stable — but Rx attach is masking a loyalty erosion signal"
Two metrics diverging. One holding the other afloat. The tension is the finding. The law of large numbers makes this expected in enterprise retail.
Mode Shift Acknowledgment

When the product detects a mode shift between responses — a change in the decision depth Alex is seeking — it opens the next response with a single sentence acknowledging the shift. This is not narration; it is alignment signaling. The VP knows the product understood their intent shift without requiring them to state it explicitly.

Mode shift acknowledgment patterns
Directional → Analytical
Triggered by: surprise language, attribution question, "why" framing after a directional response.
"Let me go deeper on this — something in that number is worth interrogating."
Analytical → Directional
Triggered by: acceptance language, short follow-on after deep attribution, delegation framing without time pressure.
"Pulling back to the story level — here's what this means for the week."
Any mode → Outcome-Focused
Triggered by: time pressure language, framing requests, planning references. Highest priority — override immediately.
"Given you're heading into planning — let me frame this for the room."
Placement rule: Mode shift acknowledgment appears as the first sentence of the response body — before the title, after the header. It is visually lighter than the title (no Playfair serif, no weight). It reads as a transition beat, not a heading. It is never more than one sentence. It disappears entirely when no mode shift is detected.
Part II Continued · Entry Modes & Decision Depth

Three Entry Modes

The product opens to a blank canvas. It may surface suggested starting questions as orientation — but the executive can bypass these entirely. Entry mode is inferred from how the first message is framed. It shapes the structure of the response. Decision mode shapes its depth. Both can shift within the same session.

Entry Mode 01
Open Exploration
Alex hasn't framed a question. May have opened the product with no input, or with a broad prompt like "show me last week." The product leads.
Response structure
Story-led. The product surfaces the most signal-bearing finding of the week first, framed with prior week context. Continuity before novelty. The product is saying: here's what I would lead with if I were briefing you — and here's how it connects to what we saw last week.
Layers typically active
Layer 1 always. Layer 2 for the lead finding. Layer 5 at the decision tier level — enough to tell Alex which tier the week's primary signal belongs to. Layers 3 and 4 available through navigation.
Navigation behavior
Strong recommendation: the most explanatory next question given the lead finding. Alternative: a different finding that may be relevant to a different pillar or decision tier. Both are offered as invitations, not prompts.
Prior week context (v1.1)
Suggested starting questions in the cold open carry prior week context. "What happened to front store trips last week — and where did we grow or shrink?" references the continuity of the week-to-week story. First response opens with a single sentence of prior week context before the title — visually lighter, integrated in body flow.
Entry Mode 02
Directed Question
Alex asks a specific question. "What happened to front store trips in urban markets last week?" The product answers that question and nothing more unless navigation pulls it further.
Response structure
Question-scoped. The product answers the question completely at the depth implied by the decision mode in the question. Does not volunteer adjacent findings. Depth before breadth. Precision is the value.
Layers typically active
Layer 1 scoped to the question. Layers 2 and 3 as needed to fully answer. Layer 4 if value quantification is available and relevant. Layer 5 abbreviated to the decision tier the question implies.
Navigation behavior
Strong recommendation: the next logical question in the attribution chain given what was just answered. Alternative: a cross-tier implication if the finding has strategic or capital relevance beyond the tactical question asked.
Entry Mode 03
Hypothesis Validation
Alex arrives with a point of view forming. "I think the trip decline is being masked by Rx attach holding steady — is there data supporting that?" The product stress-tests the hypothesis.
Response structure
Hypothesis-anchored. The product confirms, complicates, or refutes using directional evidence. The response is structured around the hypothesis — not the five-layer template. Evidence-led.
Layers typically active
Layer 1 scoped to the hypothesis population. Layer 3 is the primary layer — the directional signals that bear on the hypothesis. Layer 2 as supporting evidence. Layers 4 and 5 only if the hypothesis has a material value or decision implication.
Navigation behavior
Strong recommendation: if the hypothesis is confirmed, the next question that advances the decision. If refuted, what the data actually shows instead. Alternative: an adjacent hypothesis the data does support, if one exists.

Decision mode is inferred from the exchange — not from a setting Alex configures. The same question asked with different framing implies different depth. The product reads the mode and calibrates accordingly.

Part II Continued · Navigation Model

Generated Navigation

Navigation is the product's voice between responses. It is the mechanism by which the product demonstrates it has already thought ahead. Every correct navigation recommendation earns a unit of trust. Over time, the navigation builds the executive's confidence that the product understands the business — not just the data.

Navigation Anatomy — Two Moves Per Response
Strong Recommendation
The most consequential next move
The product's read on what would most advance Alex's decision mode. Drawn from what is most open or most load-bearing in the current response. Always offered as an invitation — never as a redirect.
Deeper into attribution — an unresolved dimension with likely signal
Across to an adjacent finding that complicates or extends this one
Upward to a higher decision tier if the finding spans tiers
Toward delegation framing if mode is directional
Alternative
A different useful direction
A genuinely different path — not a lesser version of the recommendation. Often points to a different pillar, a different decision tier, or a different entry mode. Gives Alex agency without overwhelming.
A different strategic pillar the same data speaks to
A longer-horizon implication if the week has a multi-tier signal
A hypothesis the data supports that Alex hasn't named yet
A shareable format or delegation frame if comms is imminent
Navigation Prose Standards

Navigation is always written in plain prose. It sounds like a person, not a UI. The standard is: would a brilliant analyst sitting across the table say this? If it sounds like a button label or a menu item, rewrite it.

✓ Good navigation prose
"The regional concentration behind this is worth seeing — urban stores are carrying a disproportionate share of the decline. Want me to break that down?"
"There's a quarterly signal here that may be relevant to your retention strategy review — I can frame that if it would be useful before your next planning cycle."
"If you're heading into a leadership conversation, I can give you a version of this that leads with the pillar implication rather than the metric."
✗ Navigation prose to avoid
"Click here to view regional breakdown"
"Would you like to: (a) See drivers, (b) View by region, (c) View by segment?"
"I can provide additional analysis if needed."
Navigation Mood Variants

Navigation prose adapts to the confidence state of the underlying data. Three moods are defined. The default is confident — when attribution is clean and signal is strong. Uncertain and incomplete moods surface when the data has qualification that the VP should understand before following a thread.

Confident Mood (default)
variance_state = "outside" · 6+ attribution dimensions resolved · 2+ weeks persistence
"The regional concentration is worth seeing — urban stores are carrying 68% of the decline."
Assertive, clear, no hedging. The data supports the direction.
Uncertain Mood
variance_state = "boundary" · fewer than 6 attribution dimensions resolved · single-week signal
"The regional pattern is suggestive but the signal isn't clean yet — I'd want to see one more week before reading this as structural."
Hedged, honest, invites patience. Primary item gets dashed border.
Incomplete Mood
confidence = "estimated" or "flagged" on primary metric · pending reconciliation flag
"The decile breakdown isn't fully reconciled this week — I can show you what's available, but I'd treat it as directional."
Muted, not alarming. Secondary often redirects to cleaner data path.
Analysis View Return Flow

When Alex returns from an Analysis View to the thread, the transition must feel like a conversation resuming — not like a modal closing. The return flow is a four-beat ceremony that composes the finding into the thread visually before the agent responds.

Beat 1 · Tap (0ms)
VP taps "Bring this into the conversation." Overlay slide-out begins.
Beat 2 · Compose (200ms)
User bubble types into thread at ~40ms/char. Feels like dictation appearing.
Beat 3 · Think (600ms)
Thread full width. Bubble complete. Typing indicator visible.
Beat 4 · Respond
Reintegration response appears. Opens with modal offer.
Reintegration modal offer: "You spent [N minutes] in the [view name] — want me to bring what you found into the conversation?" This is a consent moment — the VP confirms that the analysis view exploration should become conversational context.
Delegation Workflow

After establishing a finding, Alex may delegate it to an analyst for deeper investigation. The delegation creates a governed brief that travels downstream — not a chat forward, but a structured package with definitions, methodology, and scope of work. The analyst brief is an export format, not a thread continuation.

Delegation trigger
Third navigation element: "Delegate this finding →" — appears after primary and secondary nav when the finding is actionable and the context is complete enough to hand off.
Not a prompt to generate more chat. A structured export with governed definitions attached.
Brief composition
  • Finding title and pillar attribution
  • Scope of work — what Alex is asking the analyst to do
  • Governed definitions for all mentioned segments/metrics
  • Key metrics with confidence levels
  • Open hypothesis (if any) with "not causally established" label
  • What Alex investigated before delegating
Fiscal Week Session Model

The product's memory is organized by fiscal week. A session spans the FW — not the calendar day. Mid-week returns resume the session where it was left. Past fiscal weeks become read-only archives accessible from a slide-in panel.

Current FW session
Active, editable, responsive. The product has full context from all prior turns this week. Reply bar is visible. Thread is live.
Past FW archive
Read-only. The thread is viewable but not continuable. Signal elements tappable for reference — no breadcrumb events generated. "Read Only" badge visible.
Mid-week return
Session restore atom appears above thread: "You're back in FW10 — you left off looking at [context]." Auto-dismisses after 4 seconds.
Part II Continued · Decision Tier Classification

Decision Tier Framework

Every Layer 5 implication is classified by decision tier. A finding can span tiers — with distinct recommended actions at each level. The tier classification is the opportunity cost signal in the POC: it tells Alex not what is being sacrificed, but which decision window this finding belongs to and how urgent the action is.

Tier
Horizon & cadence
Decision type
Action shape
TacticalIn-week
WoW and MoM. The decision window is this week or next. Findings that require immediate operational response — promotional adjustment, inventory signal, regional intervention.
Act now or lose the window. These are decisions an operator makes, or delegates immediately to a director with a defined action.
Specific, bounded, time-sensitive. "Address lapsing urban loyalty members before end of week." Delegatable in a single instruction.
StrategicQuarterly / biannual
Multi-month programs already in motion. The finding is an input to a review or adjustment cycle — not a trigger for immediate action, but material enough to affect the next strategic decision point.
Adjust or reaffirm a multi-month strategy. Reallocate resources between pillars. Flag an emerging pattern for the next QBR or planning session.
Framing-oriented. "This finding suggests Pillar 2 retention strategy may need adjustment at next quarterly review." Requires synthesis, not immediate action.
CapitalMulti-year
5-year capital expenditure and structural business decisions. The finding is an early signal — not actionable this week — but relevant to ensuring a sustainable and growing business over a longer horizon.
Infrastructure investment, channel architecture, long-range customer portfolio strategy. These are decisions that compound over years, not weeks.
Signal-logging oriented. "This pattern, if persistent, has implications for the digital channel investment thesis in the 5-year plan." Log, monitor, revisit.

Part III · Quality Evaluation

How to Evaluate Response Completeness

A response is complete when Alex can act on it, delegate it, or decide to go deeper — without needing to reframe or translate it first. These criteria are the evaluation standard for every response the product generates.

Response Quality Criteria
01The title states a finding or tension, not a topic. "Front store trips" is a topic. "Lapsing loyalty members are driving the front store trip decline" is a finding.
02The frame is explicit — the population, metric, and time window are named before any claim is made. The response can be trusted and forwarded without the reader needing to ask "which customers?" or "what time period?"
03No causal claims. "Associated with" not "caused by." Directional statistics only. The product reports what the data shows — it does not construct an explanatory narrative beyond the signal.
04At least one strategic pillar is named and relevant to the finding. The pillar attribution is not forced — if the finding genuinely speaks to a pillar, it is named. If it doesn't, it is not.
05The decision tier is classified. Alex knows whether this is an in-week action, a quarterly signal, or a multi-year consideration — or spans tiers, with distinct actions named at each level.
Navigation Quality Criteria
01The strong recommendation is genuinely the most consequential next move — not the most obvious one. It demonstrates that the product has already considered what comes next and chosen the most useful direction.
02The alternative is a genuinely different direction — a different pillar, a different tier, a different entry mode. Not a lesser version of the recommendation.
03Both are written in plain prose that sounds like a person. Neither sounds like a button label, a menu item, or a tooltip. The test: would a brilliant analyst say this in a meeting room?
04The navigation is drawn from the response — it follows logically from what was just said. It does not introduce topics not grounded in the current response.
05Alex can follow or ignore the navigation without losing context. The thread remains intact. The next response will reference what was already established, regardless of which path Alex takes.
The Trust Test
A response passes the trust test when Alex reads it and thinks: "I could forward this as-is." Not "I could use this as a starting point." Not "I could brief my team on this if I added some context." Forward it. As written. Without a cover note.
That is the standard. Everything in this specification — the template, the entry modes, the depth calibration, the navigation model — exists in service of that single outcome. When the product earns that trust consistently, it earns the right to be the source of record.
Part IV · UI System & Signal Design

The Three-Space Layout

The UI is not a single surface. It is three distinct spaces with defined roles, each occupying the screen in proportion to what Alex is doing at any moment. The thread is always present. The sidecar and analysis view are entered and exited deliberately.

Space 01
The Thread
Primary conversational space. Always visible. Narrative responses accumulate downward. Between each product response and Alex's reply space sits the breadcrumb drawer — collapsed, anchored, expandable.
Default: full width
With sidecar: ~60% width
Analysis mode: collapses to narrow rail
Space 02
The Sidecar
Context and calibration layer. Opens on tap of a signaled element. Persists until dismissed. Updates on every tap — always showing the most recently activated element. The thread is the memory, not the sidecar.
Opens: slide-in from right
Updates: on tap, no history
Dismisses: explicit close or session end
Space 03
Analysis View
Deliberately entered mode for structured data exploration. Not a drift — an explicit door. The thread collapses to a narrow visible rail on the left. Analysis expands right. Exit returns thread to full width.
Entered: via nav prose or sidecar control
Thread: collapses to narrow rail, left
Exit: thread expands, agent updated
The boundary rule: The sidecar handles context and calibration — what a metric means, where it sits relative to baseline, how a segment is currently performing. Analysis view handles structured exploration — waterfall views, decile distributions, cohort comparisons. The sidecar never becomes a dashboard. If content warrants deeper exploration, the sidecar offers the door to analysis view explicitly. Alex walks through it deliberately.

The Breadcrumb Trail

The breadcrumb trail is the seam between conversation and investigation. It lives between the product's response and Alex's reply space, anchored to the response that generated the investigation. Collapsed by default. Expandable on demand. It does three jobs simultaneously — and must do all three without cluttering the thread.

Job 01
Agent Awareness Layer
Every sidecar tap appends a structured event to the breadcrumb trail. The agent reads these events automatically when generating the next response. Alex never has to narrate what they investigated — the agent already knows. The trail is the passive awareness mechanism.
Job 02
Investigative History
Collapsed by default and unlikely to be needed in most sessions. But when Alex needs to reconstruct their investigation path — for a debrief, a delegation note, or a backward trace through the session — the trail is there, expandable, anchored to the response that generated it.
Job 03
Modal Offer Trigger
When sidecar activity signals that Alex is in investigative depth — multiple taps in the same dimension, or engagement with a tension — the agent's next response opens with a light modal offer. Not a narration of what was seen. A door: "It looks like you've been in the regional data — do you want me to bring that into analysis view?"

Analysis View — Entry & Exit Protocol

Analysis view is not a mode Alex drifts into. It is a room they walk into deliberately and walk out of when done. The protocol governs how the transition happens, what the agent knows when Alex returns, and how the thread is preserved throughout.

Entry
Triggered by navigation prose: "I can open that in analysis view." Or by a control that appears in the sidecar when the content warrants deeper exploration.

The agent is aware of sidecar and analysis activity through the breadcrumb trail. This awareness shapes how it opens the next response. The awareness is calibrated — the agent does not narrate everything Alex looked at. It reads the investigation and responds to its pattern.

Trace — The Governed Session Object

Trace is the accumulated governed layer of a session. It is not a log of what Alex said — it is a record of what the governed data produced in response to Alex's investigation. Every metric tap, entity tap, tension tap, and analysis view entry automatically adds an event to Trace. By the time a session produces a finding worth delegating, Trace is already complete.

Trace differs from the breadcrumb trail in a critical way. The breadcrumb trail is Alex's investigation path — it tells the agent what Alex has been doing so the next response can be shaped accordingly. Trace is the governed data layer that made each finding trustworthy — it carries metric definitions, MECE decomposition, confidence levels, source, and reconciliation timestamps. The breadcrumb trail is for the agent. Trace is for the analyst.

Layout Contract — Where Trace Lives
Primary — Reply Bar (atom-trace-indicator)
The Trace Indicator atom lives in the left side of the reply bar. It appears on the first investigative event and grows with each subsequent one. This is where Alex's attention is during an active session — the indicator is in the flow, not outside it.
Tapping the indicator opens the Trace Panel organism, which slides up from the reply bar. Alex reviews the accumulated items, optionally edits the scope of work, and initiates delegation from within this panel.
The indicator never resets within a session. If Alex delegates and continues investigating, Trace continues accumulating for the next potential delegation.
Secondary — Topbar (existing Trace nav link)
The existing Trace link in the topbar remains as a secondary access path. It opens the same Trace Panel organism. This serves Alex when returning to a session mid-week and wanting to review what was previously traced — a different cognitive moment than in-flow delegation.
The topbar link is not the primary affordance. The reply bar indicator is. If Alex knows to look in the topbar, the feature works — but it should not be the only way to find it.
Delegate — The Governed Handoff

Delegate is the mechanism by which Alex transfers a governed finding to an analyst without transcription risk. The product owns the handoff. Alex's role is to provide scope context and name the recipient. Everything else — the definitions, the MECE data, the store IDs, the confidence levels, the data lineage — is assembled from Trace automatically and delivered as a structured Analyst Brief.

The current state — Alex reads the product, takes notes or screenshots, sends an email — introduces three manual steps and the transcription risk that comes with them. Delegate replaces all three with a single confirm action. The brief the analyst receives is the governed layer, not Alex's interpretation of it.

The Handoff Gap — Before and After
Before · Current state
Alex reads the governed response. The governed layer — definitions, MECE, confidence — is visible to Alex in the sidecar but not transmitted.
Alex takes notes, screenshots, or paraphrases. Each step introduces potential transcription error.
Alex sends an email with the prose summary. The analyst receives a paragraph — no definitions, no store IDs, no confidence levels, no methodology.
The analyst must run a clarification loop before work can begin. The governed data layer that made the finding trustworthy is invisible to the person executing on it.
After · Delegate flow
Alex investigates normally. Trace accumulates automatically from every sidecar interaction and analysis view entry.
Alex taps the Trace Indicator, reviews the accumulated items, adds a scope note, selects the analyst.
The product generates and delivers the Analyst Brief — governed definitions, MECE decomposition, store IDs, confidence levels, data lineage, investigation trace.
The analyst receives a governed document and can begin work immediately. No clarification loop. The compounding value of the governed layer reaches the execution layer.

What the Analyst Receives

The Analyst Brief (tmpl-analyst-brief) is structured, not prose. The analyst can navigate directly to the section they need — definitions, store list, investigation trace — without reading top-to-bottom. The brief is designed for the analyst's workflow, not Alex's narrative.

What the analyst can do
Pull the lapsing segment from the data warehouse using the governed definition — without a clarification call. The 30% frequency decline threshold, the 90-day lookback, the 30-day window: all in the brief.
What the analyst no longer needs
A clarification meeting to understand what "lapsing" means. A second email to get the store list. A question about whether the $2.2M figure is modeled or measured. All of this is in the brief.
What the brief does not include
Alex's conversation thread. Alex's investigation path. The product's reasoning. The brief contains the governed data layer only — not the session that produced it. The analyst is a governed consumer, not a session spectator.
